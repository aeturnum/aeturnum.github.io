It’s common to describe written works as a ‘text’ - a transparent technology that transmits the content of language into the mind of the reader. In this imagining different copies of a written work are interchangeable because they all mean the same thing. The arrangement of the characters, their rendering and the written medium are all subservient to the content. This view is wrong. Whenever someone encounters a written work, they encounter it rendered in a particular way. As an image. The idea of ‘text’ (or ğŸ…£ğŸ…”ğŸ…§ğŸ…£) can emerge in our minds only once we read that written work. Every individual written work is a series of images which use genre, convention and social practice to guide semiotic translation. How does this (Fig 1) message exchange read to you? How do you read the response? To me, the response ("Mom") feels assertive and definitive. A declaration in periods. A refusal to react to what is probably a larger situation. Some of what Mom means would be clear in any medium, but extra confines of messaging (timed arrival, division of writing between messages)1 matter here. Short messages with full stops might mean anger, withholding or judgment. How much Mom intended to transmit those emotions is an open question.

Much of the tension of this exchange rests in how what we see interacts with how the viewer likely knows messaging works. Whatever “happened” is just above what we can see, available to both participants but not to us. Contextually, this exchange is obviously incomplete, but the reason we know is the visual character of the presentation. Fig 2 has the same exchange rendered using the conventions of a screenplay instead of a text message conversation.

Of course, this exchange looks different! It doesn’t just look different: the difference in looks suggests different meanings. Even though the ğŸ…£ğŸ…”ğŸ…§ğŸ…£ hasn’t changed at all. Skeptical readers of this piece might suggest that the differences in interpretation come down to genre of text. But, of course, Marcel Mauss observed that the way that we come to understand the genre of things is basically visual. We can tell someone is giving a talk because one person is sitting and talking and the others are sitting quietly and listening.2 The meaning of the text depends on identification of its genre, something that cannot be done without understanding how the text was visually presented.

Are “ooooook” and “Ok” same word? You won’t find the version with six ‘o’s in any dictionary. This is not a situation where repeating letters changes the dictionary definition (as in ‘lose’ and ‘loose’), but where adding letters changes the sense of affect that wraps the linguistic portion of the message. The affect of what we say is as important, if not more important, than the words we chose. Emphasizing the first syllable in “ok” can be playful, it can express exasperation or exhaustion. It can communicate skepticism. When we speak, these ideas are communicated with tone and body language, but when we write we’re limited. Books must be printed from a (decreasingly) fixed set of characters, so we use text’s version of body language: departures from what the writer (and hopefully the reader) understand as the standards of ‘proper’ writing.

Like the spoken work, the difference between these two visual renderings exists, to a great deal, outside of written language as such. They manipulate their visual character to invoke a shared understanding of spoken language that accompanies it.

The central fiction of written material is that it uncomplicatedly links a thought to a word through the written page. That when a word is written it is simply the word. A transparent representation of an idea. This is obviously incorrect if one stops to consider it. Just as it is impossible to separate the written word from visual and cultural affect as it is to separate the spoken word from its embodied and affective qualities.

Thankfully, far from harming the power of ğŸ…£ğŸ…”ğŸ…§ğŸ…£, the understanding that writing is always an image first opens up new ways to ‘read’ texts as images.

A Social Image

Before analyzing the visual qualities of writing creates ğŸ…£ğŸ…”ğŸ…§ğŸ…£, we must first consider the troublingly similar visual layout of text. This, too, is visual. Changing the visual presentation of “a text” will change how that text is read. The formatting choices for each form are both functional and semiotic.

Text messages are always carried on between yourself and one or more others. The other(s) are always on the left, you are always on the right. In conversations where there is more than two speakers, each speaker’s messages will often have a different background color. The work of identifying the speaker that novels do in writing is implied by a label at the top of buffer of text messages. Nothing in the format names the speaker of the messages on the right. The authorship is implied by the structure and so mainly exists in the ğŸ…£ğŸ…”ğŸ…§ğŸ…£ that exists in our minds.

There are functional elements of the form. It takes advantage of the fact that text exchanges are always between “you” and some number of others to efficiently omit redundant information. Adding the information that’s included in the format of scripts (copious space, different formats for speaking and description) would make text messages less functional. There are qualities (affordances) of the format that correspond to the demands of the format.

There are also semiotic qualities of the form. Certain kinds of exchanges happen over text messages and so the use of the form communicates how the exchange should be read. Direct experience of text messages (or some kind of messaging) is essential to understanding the experiential qualities of the form. As much as one can explain “what it is like” to send or receive text messages, those descriptions will always use other experiences as reference.3 I might say that it’s like exchanging short letters - but of course, in important ways, it is not. The semiotic quality of format always comes back to our ability to recognize experiential qualities that someone is trying to signal. The functional aspects of the format of text messaging help new and experienced users, but it’s only experienced users who will understand the semiotics.

When Fig 4 imagines characters exchanging text messages, it imagines them speaking differently than they do in the book. But this is not the same as encountering writing-as-image. For Fig 4 to make sense, the viewer must know what a text messages is, they must understand that these characters are expressing themselves in the context of a text message exchange. In a sense, the format of a text message exchange is a visual container that communicates context. Its value comes in its ability to communicate genre and so it cannot be used as a mode of expression, except insofar as the mode of expression involves playing with the genre itself. Fig 4 only makes sense to an audience who has read Pride and Prejudice and and who knows how they express themselves differently in text messages and in speech.

The visual character of writing has can only be understood only within these genres of presentation (or presentation genre). To see how this is the case, try to imagine writing outside of a presentation genre. Handwritten scrawl, letters cut out and pasted together (a’la ransom notes), and street art are all presentation genres. As when people create a new genre of presentation (“the email”, “the snap”, “the text message”) part of that creation is an act of separation between it and other forms of communication. Like modern photography, these presentation genres are part of how we exchange messages with each other.4

Once the presentation genre is established, then the visual work of writing can begin. Fig 5 is a page from a 1524 edition of the Tanakh5. The central columns are the text itself, but the many notes around it are commentaries on the central text itself.6 The spatial relationships between the text are not an accident. Placing the actual scripture at the center of the page and the commentaries around it reflect the power relationship between the writings. Each page centers around the scripture - it’s why the reader and the Rabbis who wrote the commentary are here. But it would be wrong to say that the relationship is one way. The commentaries selected for this text are two of many possible commentaries and presenting them in this way, physically supporting a sacred scripture, elevates these commentaries in particular.

Here we begin to see writing-as-image. This book is more than the sum of its parts. It constructs a different ğŸ…£ğŸ…”ğŸ…§ğŸ…£ in the mind than reading the Tanakh on its own with the two commentaries in their own. Looking at a page of this copy of the Tanakh means needing to also look at the commentaries sharing space with the scripture of the Tanakh. It also means seeing how the explanations and interpretations wrap the scripture - performing the same role to the reader as the Rabbit does for the observant - opening up the scripture and suggesting more readings. It suggests that one not need understand the holy scripture alone, that reading interpretations is good and right and helpful. It says all this by simply arranging the texts in relation to one another.

It’s important to note that this effect is created solely through the arrangement of the characters. The font used for the scripture and the commentaries are the same.7 This is, in its own right, another social message. In the same way that presenting the texts together, using the same font to render them suggests they exist (in some sense) on the same level. The way writing is arranged on a page is one level of visuality, but not the level of visuality that this essay focuses on. Writing arrangement is too closely related and dependent on the presentation genre - if Fig 5 were to lose its genre of Tanakh, all the work the arrangement is doing would also be lost. Many arrangements of texts are impossible in a particular genre. It’s hard to imagine constructing a text message version of the Tanakh with commentary. Which “side” is the commentary on? Which side is the Tanakh? The balanced structural arrangement in Fig 5 that suggests the commentaries are co-equal would likely be impossible.

The real meat of the visuality in writing lies in the characters themselves. Character sets have deep cultural and temporal resonance that ultimately flow from their character as images. This should be clear to any reader who was alive during the early 2000s because they were around for the rise of the Emoji. Some people feel like Emojis aren’t “really” writing, but I claim it’s the oppsite: all characters are images first and become ğŸ…£ğŸ…”ğŸ…§ğŸ…£ in our minds.
