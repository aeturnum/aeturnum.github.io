It’s common to describe written works as a ‘text’ - a transparent technology that transmits the content of language into the mind of the reader. In this imagining different copies of a written work are interchangeable because they all mean the same thing. The arrangement of the characters, their rendering and the written medium are all subservient to the content. This view is wrong. Whenever someone encounters a written work, they encounter it rendered in a particular way. As an image. The idea of ‘text’ (or 🅣🅔🅧🅣) can emerge in our minds only once we read that written work. Every individual written work is a series of images which use genre, convention and social practice to guide semiotic translation. The idea that 🅣🅔🅧🅣, which existing only in the mind, dominates the meaning of written works is incorrect. The understanding of 🅣🅔🅧🅣 is always guided by the visual encounter with the written word.

How does this (Fig 1) message exchange read to you? How do you read the response? To me, the response ("Mom") feels assertive and definitive. A declaration in periods. A refusal to react to what is probably a larger situation. Some of what Mom means would be clear in any medium, but extra confines of messaging (timed arrival, division of writing between messages)1 matter here. Short messages with full stops might mean anger, withholding or judgment. How much Mom intended to transmit those emotions is an open question.

Much of the tension of this exchange rests in how what we see interacts with how the viewer likely knows messaging works. Whatever “happened” is just above what we can see, available to both participants but not to us. Contextually, this exchange is obviously incomplete, but the reason we know is the visual character of the presentation. Of course, this exchange looks different! It doesn’t just look different: the difference in looks suggests different meanings. Even though the 🅣🅔🅧🅣 hasn’t changed at all. Skeptical readers of this piece might suggest that the differences in interpretation come down to genre of text. But, of course, Marcel Mauss observed that the way that we come to understand the genre of things is basically visual. We can tell someone is giving a talk because one person is sitting and talking and the others are sitting quietly and listening.2 The meaning of the text depends on identification of its genre, something that cannot be done without understanding how the text was visually presented.

If we change the 🅣🅔🅧🅣 a little we find another rude intrusion from the visual.

Are “ooooook” and “Ok” same word? You won’t find the version with six ‘o’s in any dictionary. This is not a situation where repeating letters changes the dictionary definition (as in ‘lose’ and ‘loose’), but where adding letters changes the sense of affect that wraps the linguistic portion of the message. The affect of what we say is as important, if not more important, than the words we chose. Emphasizing the first syllable in “ok” can be playful, it can express exasperation or exhaustion. It can communicate skepticism. When we speak, these ideas are communicated with tone and body language, but when we write we’re limited. Books must be printed from a (decreasingly) fixed set of characters, so we use text’s version of body language: departures from what the writer (and hopefully the reader) understand as the standards of ‘proper’ writing.

Like the spoken work, the difference between these two visual renderings exists, to a great deal, outside of written language as such. They manipulate their visual character to invoke a shared understanding of spoken language that accompanies it.

🅣🅔🅧🅣

The central fiction of written material is that it uncomplicatedly links a thought to a word through the written page. That when a word is written it is simply the word. A transparent representation of an idea. This is obviously incorrect if one stops to consider it. Just as it is impossible to separate the written word from visual and cultural affect as it is to separate the spoken word from its embodied and affective qualities.

Thankfully, far from harming the power of 🅣🅔🅧🅣, the understanding that writing is always an image first opens up new ways to ‘read’ texts as images.

A Social Image

Before analyzing the visual qualities of writing creates 🅣🅔🅧🅣, we must first consider the troublingly similar visual layout of text. This, too, is visual. Changing the visual presentation of “a text” will change how that text is read. The formatting choices for each form are both functional and semiotic.

Text messages are always carried on between yourself and one or more others. The other(s) are always on the left, you are always on the right. In conversations where there is more than two speakers, each speaker’s messages will often have a different background color. The work of identifying the speaker that novels do in writing is implied by a label at the top of buffer of text messages. Nothing in the format names the speaker of the messages on the right. The authorship is implied by the structure and so mainly exists in the 🅣🅔🅧🅣 that exists in our minds.

There are functional elements of the form. It takes advantage of the fact that text exchanges are always between “you” and some number of others to efficiently omit redundant information. Adding the information that’s included in the format of scripts (copious space, different formats for speaking and description) would make text messages less functional. There are qualities (affordances) of the format that correspond to the demands of the format.

There are also semiotic qualities of the form. Certain kinds of exchanges happen over text messages and so the use of the form communicates how the exchange should be read. Direct experience of text messages (or some kind of messaging) is essential to understanding the experiential qualities of the form. As much as one can explain “what it is like” to send or receive text messages, those descriptions will always use other experiences as reference.3 I might say that it’s like exchanging short letters - but of course, in important ways, it is not. The semiotic quality of format always comes back to our ability to recognize experiential qualities that someone is trying to signal. The functional aspects of the format of text messaging help new and experienced users, but it’s only experienced users who will understand the semiotics.

When Fig 4 imagines characters exchanging text messages, it imagines them speaking differently than they do in the book. But this is not the same as encountering writing-as-image. For Fig 4 to make sense, the viewer must know what a text messages is, they must understand that these characters are expressing themselves in the context of a text message exchange. In a sense, the format of a text message exchange is a visual container that communicates context. Its value comes in its ability to communicate genre and so it cannot be used as a mode of expression, except insofar as the mode of expression involves playing with the genre itself. Fig 4 only makes sense to an audience who has read Pride and Prejudice and and who knows how they express themselves differently in text messages and in speech.

The visual character of writing has can only be understood only within these genres of presentation (or presentation genre). To see how this is the case, try to imagine writing outside of a presentation genre. Handwritten scrawl, letters cut out and pasted together (a’la ransom notes), and street art are all presentation genres. As when people create a new genre of presentation (“the email”, “the snap”, “the text message”) part of that creation is an act of separation between it and other forms of communication. Like modern photography, these presentation genres are part of how we exchange messages with each other.4

Once the presentation genre is established, then the visual work of writing can begin. 🖼5 is a page from a 1524 edition of the Tanakh5. The central columns are the text itself, but the many notes around it are commentaries on the central text itself.6 The spatial relationships between the text are not an accident. Placing the actual scripture at the center of the page and the commentaries around it reflect the power relationship between the writings. Each page centers around the scripture - it’s why the reader and the Rabbis who wrote the commentary are here. But it would be wrong to say that the relationship is one way. The commentaries selected for this text are two of many possible commentaries and presenting them in this way, physically supporting a sacred scripture, elevates these commentaries in particular.

Here we begin to see writing-as-image. This book is more than the sum of its parts. It constructs a different 🅣🅔🅧🅣 in the mind than reading the Tanakh on its own with the two commentaries in their own. Looking at a page of this copy of the Tanakh means needing to also look at the commentaries sharing space with the scripture of the Tanakh. It also means seeing how the explanations and interpretations wrap the scripture - performing the same role to the reader as the Rabbit does for the observant - opening up the scripture and suggesting more readings. It suggests that one not need understand the holy scripture alone, that reading interpretations is good and right and helpful. It says all this by simply arranging the texts in relation to one another.

It’s important to note that this effect is created solely through the arrangement of the characters. The font used for the scripture and the commentaries are the same.7 This is, in its own right, another social message. In the same way that presenting the texts together, using the same font to render them suggests they exist (in some sense) on the same level. The way writing is arranged on a page is one level of visuality, but not the level of visuality that this essay focuses on. Writing arrangement is too closely related and dependent on the presentation genre - if Fig 5 were to lose its genre of Tanakh, all the work the arrangement is doing would also be lost. Many arrangements of texts are impossible in a particular genre. It’s hard to imagine constructing a text message version of the Tanakh with commentary. Which “side” is the commentary on? Which side is the Tanakh? The balanced structural arrangement in Fig 5 that suggests the commentaries are co-equal would likely be impossible.

The real meat of the visuality in writing lies in the characters themselves. Character sets have deep cultural and temporal resonance that ultimately flow from their character as images. This should be clear to any reader who was alive during the early 2000s because they were around for the rise of the emoji. Some people feel like emojis aren’t “really” writing, but I claim it’s the opposite: all characters are images first and become 🅣🅔🅧🅣 in our minds.

🅴🅼🅾🅹🅸

As opposed to traditional writing, where each letter (or word for pictographic languages) is an image that represents a linguistic sign, 🅴🅼🅾🅹🅸 are images that represent a thing or an idea. 🅴🅼🅾🅹🅸 admit that it’s appropriate to mix writing with images (because writing has always been a series of images). Each 🅴🅼🅾🅹🅸 has a written description that’s selected and voted on by the Unicode Consortium. Then individual 🅴🅼🅾🅹🅸 providers produce their own version of each 🅴🅼🅾🅹🅸. In this process, cultural biases that are so effectively hidden by the 🅣🅔🅧🅣 myth come forward - like when Apple chose to represent the “Dancer” 🅴🅼🅾🅹🅸 as a female flamenco dancer.8

The rise in prominence of the 🅴🅼🅾🅹🅸🆂 reflects the demands of a society that increasingly uses text-based communication as a substitute for (as opposed to as a supplement of) in-person communication. With the presentation format of a written work already used to stabilize the understanding of how one should read the work, 🅴🅼🅾🅹🅸 expand the limited power of written images9. The diversity of representation for the same 🅴🅼🅾🅹🅸 and the separation of 🅴🅼🅾🅹🅸 set selection from font selection shows the diversity of approaches and philosophies towards the question of representing the symbols of non-glyphs.

✊⏰

The written word enjoys an unusually strong claim to be representing language. Photographs and paintings have struggled for years about the question of representation. Though a photograph and a painting are objects in themselves, they also are pictures of things.10 The connection between the objects that we can see and touch in the world and the objects that appear in images feels especially strong in these mediums where both painting and photographs are related to objects that have existed.11 Photographs are the product of recording reflected light and while paintings don’t need to depict real objects, people expect them to link back to the real world - to be a painting “of” something. Barthes described the difficulty of seeing a photo over the thing the photo is “of”12: the object and the photo are entangled together. The subject of a famous photo will always be associated with that photo: seen in a particular way and from a particular angle. The photo, which could be imagined as being the insight which gives us access to the subject, is also seen as hopelessly dependent on the subject. This two-way-relationship that exists between the depicted and the depicting comes to be important in the visual character of writing.

When a young girl visiting Agnes Martin’s home picked a rose, Martin asked her if the rose was beautiful. The girl said it was. Martin hid the rose behind her back and asked if the rose was still beautiful. Her visitor replied yes again. “You see,” Martin said, “beauty is in your mind, not in the rose."13 The beauty is 🅣🅔🅧🅣.

Painting of the abstract is powerful because we know there are things that do not have a fixed visual quality. “Friendship” is not, of course, a picture of friendship. Instead, it is a picture that might hope to evoke in the viewer a similar feeling as the viewer feels when they experience friendship. Depictings of letters and paintings of feelings have something in common: they are both pictures of things that don’t have physical form. When we draw or sculpt or carve a letter, we are depicting something, like friendship, that doesn’t have a physical form. Instead it is a social and cultural agreement to depict an aspect of language (a language-idea) in a recognizable and reproducible way.

So the two-way-relationship that exists between letter and language-idea has a difference balance of power. The language-idea has a purposely unfixed form, impacted by accent, culture, experience, mood and many other things aside. Each person’s experience of a language-idea is deeply personal. It’s why we feel an immediate kinship if someone says (or writes) something that suggests they share a cultural background (it’s also why that feeling is reliable). Hyperobjects are things that are too large for any human to fully see and understand their effect.14 Global warming is one, but culture is another. The language-idea exists within a hyperobject of culture and the two-way-relationship between it and the letter is experienced affectively. The character is still a visual object and that visual character is important (as we will see), but its cultural resonance is central to its ability to represent.

This places writing in a strange representational position. Much like presentation genre it could be stuck between dutiful conformity to expectations and incomprehensible illegibility. When he spoke about the qualities of photography, Barthes highlighted how photography inescapably was taken at a time and that the photography carried that time with it. He did not mean that it carried the date as such, but that the character and situation of a time in history is unavoidably depicted in images of it.

The script used in 🖼🤡 is called “Fraktur” (𝕱𝖗𝖆𝖐𝖙𝖚𝖗). It was the official font of the Nazis movement in 1930’s Germany. The font was not created by the Nazis - it was based on a lettering technique created to deal with the challenges of reliably hand-writing letters (painting images of letters) by scribes. It became heavily associated with “Germanness” before the Nazis movement and was adopted by the movement in their faux promotion of everything “german.” Today this font is irrevocably associated with fascism and racism.15

Even if one accepts the idea that particular characters (or particular fonts) can connect a piece of writing to a particular cultural hyperobject, there’s a question of specificity. Are fonts specific? Is it 𝕱𝖗𝖆𝖐𝖙𝖚𝖗-in-itself that represents fascism, or is it how 𝕱𝖗𝖆𝖐𝖙𝖚𝖗 looks16? If writing is not read as an image first, but is directly accessed as a symbol, than visual confusion shouldn’t be a concern. The human “reader” will see the symbol of the text: be it the fascistic 𝕱𝖗𝖆𝖐𝖙𝖚𝖗 or the benign (modern) Blackletter font.

Its fascistic quality is 🅣🅔🅧🅣 in both their minds. What the font represents depends on the cultural hyperobject it’s connected to, and while people of another era might have been able to see the difference, modern people cannot.

The difference in the world were 𝕱𝖗𝖆𝖐𝖙𝖚𝖗 was the official font of a fascist government and the world where an incompetent fascist picks the wrong text to signal his racism brings us back to a quality of images: they have both a visual and cultural character. As I said earlier, 𝕱𝖗𝖆𝖐𝖙𝖚𝖗 characters link back to a particular cultural hyperobject, but not all seers will perceive that link. Over time, images that made sense to those who made them become Surreal. They do this not because the images were surreal at their creation, but because surreality emerges out of the combination of how we imagine something should be represented and how we see it represented before us.18

It’s not surreal because time has passed, but because we are re-introducing the history of a font (and the calligraphic style from which it descends). Instead of time revealing surreality though its passage, it reveals surreality by re-introducing a context around 𝕱𝖗𝖆𝖐𝖙𝖚𝖗 that @brandon_couture_ was almost certainly not focusing on when they produced this piece of art.

Crucially, the production of surreality does not depend on intent or semiotics. When Sontag designated Ghitta Carell’s portraits of the Mussolini era surreal it was because the difference in her cultural frame and Carell’s. It was how that difference revealed the absurdity of wealth and pretense in the brief, vainglorious regime and the temporary and illusory nature of the airs of the regime. Carell does not need to have been “in on it.”

In the same way, the surreality of @brandon_couture_‘s image exists as 🅣🅔🅧🅣 in the minds of those who are primed to see it. The image is a product of our times, where displaying ones artistry on Instagram is common and the specter of fascism seems far away to some. There’s no reason to believe the intent behind the image was surreal in character. The surreality exists in the image. Specifically in the surreal image of the writing itself.

When is a 🚬 Just a 🚬?

Barthes introduced two terms when he was explaining his personal experience experience of photos. Barthes tries very hard to say that these terms are meaningful only to him, but like many people before me I will ignore him. He uses the term studium to talk about a kind of average quality shared between images of a type.19 Second, there’s the punctum, the detail that leaps out of the image that strikes an individual viewer in a particular way. The punctum cannot be intentionally inserted into the image to provoke interest. An image might have many punctum for different viewers, but its studium would be fixed.

These concepts apply as well to writing as they do to other images. A good font will be all studium and no punctum - all average, with nothing standing out to the viewer at all. This is how writing is thought to work: a smooth transmission of linguistic ideas. That what you see when you look at writing is a 🅣🅔🅧🅣 behind the writing. Of course, you literally see the writing on the page, but its character shouldn’t intrude into . But what would text with a punctum even look like?

Comic Sans (𝕱𝖎𝖌 𝕮𝖔𝖒𝖎𝖈 𝕾𝖆𝖓𝖘) is a famously hated font. There’s a long parade of articles explaining why it is a bad font, or inappropriate. Whatever the specifics, the authors are revealing that, for them, Comic Sans contains a punctum. Just like Barthes’ photographic punctum, this one is deeply personal and becomes more powerful when the creator of the image doesn’t intend to create a punctum. When their intended 🅣🅔🅧🅣 omits the cultural baggage of Comic Sans. When someone uses Comic Sans with the intent of provoking those who dislike comic sans, it’s done with a wink towards the target audience. If that ‘wink’ is effectively read, the reader reacts to the Comic Sans image as a work of parody and the font loses its punctum. If the ‘wink’ isn’t read, then the punctum remains precisely because the author tried to produce parody but failed.

It’s important to draw a distinction between the cultural association of 𝕱𝖗𝖆𝖐𝖙𝖚𝖗 and the punctum of Comic Sans. 𝕱𝖗𝖆𝖐𝖙𝖚𝖗 is fascistic because it was used by the Nazi party, not because its visual character is inherently fascistic in some sense. That cultural connection is established through the act of seeing 𝕱𝖗𝖆𝖐𝖙𝖚𝖗, but because 𝕱𝖗𝖆𝖐𝖙𝖚𝖗 is intended as a shorthand, it cannot be be a punctum. Comic Sans has understood qualities (being unserious, lightness, silliness) that come from shared cultural context (like 𝕱𝖗𝖆𝖐𝖙𝖚𝖗). Comic Sans’ punctum comes from the person applying it being unaware of the cultural association.

When Unicode originally added the 🔫 🅴🅼🅾🅹🅸 in 2011, the description was simply “pistol” and the sample image provided by the organization seemed similar to a pistol that fires bullets (a firearm).20 In all likelihood, when you see this essay, you will see a 🔫 that looks more like a squirt pistol. 🖼🔫 shows the old and new Apple character for 🔫.

When 🔫 was added, it was added to the Unicode section for representing “tools."21 Arguably, a firearm pistol makes more sense as a “tool” than a squirt pistol. Hard to imagine hunting for food with a squirt pistol. The change in what kind of pistol 🔫 represents was in response to the punctum of the old 🔫. The punctum of 🔫 is a threat, but as before it’s only a punctum when the threat isn’t intended. Those seeking to threaten violence don’t need 🔫 to display a firearm pistol, they can use 🔪 or 🗡 or ✊ or ol’ fashioned written language. They can craft a message to create the desired final 🅣🅔🅧🅣 in the mind of the reader. The problem comes back to unexpected readings, or a punctum. The danger was threatening someone when you didn’t mean to.

Where traditional fonts link an image to a language-idea, 🅴🅼🅾🅹🅸 link an image to a thing-idea. Like the language-idea a thing-idea is influenced by social experience of the idea, but it’s also influenced by the qualities of the thing the character seeks to represent. Unlike the language-idea, which doesn’t exist outside of the shared mental 🅣🅔🅧🅣 of language, object 🅴🅼🅾🅹🅸🆂 exist outside of language and our minds. When Apple wanted to avoid a punctum they were restricted in which objects they could depict as 🔫 by the physical existence of “pistol.”

So does this bring us back to where we were with representation in photographs and paintings? With the represented object and the image irreversibly entangled? Not quite. This is more like the work of Agnes Marten. 🅴🅼🅾🅹🅸 are not images of things, they are images of the idea of things. They are images of how things exist in our minds. This is reflected in the change in the 🔫 emoji: it’s better that 🅴🅼🅾🅹🅸 avoid too much specificity. In a way, the punctum of 🔫 came from the specific kind of pistol depicted. Because the new 🔫 is less commonly seen, it also more cleanly stands in for a diversity of pistols. The images’ obscurity lends itself to its association with a greater range of things. This is the studium of 🅴🅼🅾🅹🅸🆂.

So what difference does it make to type “cigarette” or “🚬”? Maybe a better question is what’s the difference between “𝔠𝔦𝔤𝔞𝔯𝔢𝔱𝔱𝔢” and “🚬”? As I’ve tried to argue, the question is not between “text” and image, but between different kinds of visual communication. After all, “cigarette” links its letters to a language-idea, but then that language-idea is linked to a thing-idea. A 🚬. The 🚬 is more direct. It skips a link between the “page” (web or otherwise) and the 🅣🅔🅧🅣 that we carry around with us once we’ve read something.

⏭🔜🅣🅔🅧🅣

Writing is relentlessly, unavoidably visual. That this truth is rarely considered is a testament to the strength of presentation-genre and the work of maintaining the studium of writing styles. But we are now in an age of an explosion of flexibility in writing. This document is published in a medium that allows the reader to set their own font, use their own set of 🅴🅼🅾🅹🅸🆂. This will increasingly be way people access written works of all kinds. An explosion in diversity of the visual character of writing. An explosion in the diversity of paths taken to arrive at the mental 🅣🅔🅧🅣.

This essay lays out some of the visual tools writers can use to shape the impact of their texts. Be aware that presentation can evoke (or suppress) surreality. Consider using an 🅴🅼🅾🅹🅸 to be more direct (even though you aren’t quite sure what image your reader may see). When the Tanakh in 🖼5 was written, new fonts were hugely expensive. We now live in a golden age of visual writing and we should embrace it.
