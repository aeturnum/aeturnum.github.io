It’s common to describe written works as a ‘text’ - a transparent technology that transmits the content of language into the mind of the reader. In this imagining different copies of a written work are interchangeable because they all mean the same thing. The arrangement of the characters, their rendering and the written medium are all subservient to the content. This view is wrong. Whenever someone encounters a written work, they encounter it rendered in a particular way. As an image. Skeptical readers of this piece might suggest that the differences in interpretation come down to genre of text. But, of course, Marcel Mauss observed that the way that we come to understand the genre of things is basically visual. We can tell someone is giving a talk because one person is sitting and talking and the others are sitting quietly and listening.2 The meaning of the text depends on identification of its genre, something that cannot be done without understanding how the text was visually presented.

Are “ooooook” and “Ok” same word? You won’t find the version with six ‘o’s in any dictionary. This is not a situation where repeating letters changes the dictionary definition (as in ‘lose’ and ‘loose’), but where adding letters changes the sense of affect that wraps the linguistic portion of the message. The affect of what we say is as important, if not more important, than the words we chose. Emphasizing the first syllable in “ok” can be playful, it can express exasperation or exhaustion. It can communicate skepticism. When we speak, these ideas are communicated with tone and body language, but when we write we’re limited. Books must be printed from a (decreasingly) fixed set of characters, so we use text’s version of body language: departures from what the writer (and hopefully the reader) understand as the standards of ‘proper’ writing.

Like the spoken work, the difference between these two visual renderings exists, to a great deal, outside of written language as such. They manipulate their visual character to invoke a shared understanding of spoken language that accompanies it.

The central fiction of written material is that it uncomplicatedly links a thought to a word through the written page. That when a word is written it is simply the word. A transparent representation of an idea. This is obviously incorrect if one stops to consider it. Just as it is impossible to seperate the written word from visual and cultural affect as it is to seperate the spoken word from its embodied and affective qualities.
